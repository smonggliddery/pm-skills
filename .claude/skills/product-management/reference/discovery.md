# Discovery

How to learn what's true about your customers, their problems, and whether your solution matters. Discovery is not validation theater - it's truth-seeking.

---

## The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

The core rule: talk about their life, not your idea. People will lie to you - not maliciously, but to be polite. Design conversations that even your mum can't lie to you about.

### The Three Rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Ask about what they already do, not what they'd hypothetically do with your thing.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or hypotheticals.** "Tell me about the last time you..." beats "Do you usually..." every time.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** You're there to learn, not to pitch.

### Questions That Work

- "Tell me about the last time you [did the thing]..."
- "What happened next?"
- "How are you dealing with that today?"
- "What else have you tried?"
- "How much time/money does that cost you?"
- "Who else is involved in that decision?"
- "What would happen if you didn't solve this?"

### Questions That Fail

- "Would you use a product that...?" (Hypothetical - they'll say yes to be nice)
- "Do you think this is a good idea?" (Fishing for compliments)
- "How much would you pay for...?" (Hypothetical pricing is fiction)
- "Would you recommend this to a friend?" (Socially desirable answer)

### The Three Types of Bad Data

1. **Compliments**: "That sounds amazing!" - feels good, means nothing. Deflect compliments and return to their life.
2. **Fluff**: Generics ("I usually..."), hypotheticals ("I would..."), future promises ("I'd definitely buy that"). All worthless.
3. **Ideas**: "You should add a feature that..." - the feature request without context. Dig into the underlying problem instead.

### Commitment and Advancement

Real signal comes from commitment, not words:
- **Time commitment**: They schedule a follow-up, introduce you to someone, agree to a pilot
- **Reputation commitment**: They put their name behind you to colleagues or their boss
- **Financial commitment**: They pre-order, sign a letter of intent, or put down a deposit

If you leave a conversation with only compliments and no commitments, you learned nothing.

### Keeping It Casual

The best learning happens in conversations, not formal interviews. Structured interview settings trigger performance mode. Casual conversations reveal truth.

## Jobs-to-Be-Done (Clayton Christensen)

Customers don't buy products - they hire them to make progress in specific circumstances. Innovation succeeds when you understand the job, not the customer demographic.

### Core Concepts

**The Job**: The progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. Not a feature need, not a demographic attribute - a struggle in context.

**Functional, emotional, and social dimensions**: Every job has all three. The functional job (get to work) comes with emotional (feel safe) and social (don't look foolish) dimensions. Products that nail all three win.

**Hiring and firing**: Customers hire a product when it does the job better than current alternatives. They fire it when something better comes along or the job changes. "Why did they hire us?" is more useful than "who are our users?"

### Finding Jobs

**Look for struggling moments** - places where people are:
- Working around limitations of existing solutions
- Cobbling together multiple tools to get something done
- Abandoning a process partway through
- Spending way more time or money than the task warrants
- Not consuming at all (non-consumption is a massive signal)

**Circumstance matters more than persona**: The same person hires different products in different circumstances. A CEO might hire a complex analytics tool at work and a simple spreadsheet for personal budgeting. Segment by circumstance, not demographics.

**Competing against non-consumption**: Your biggest competitor is often "nothing" - people choosing not to solve the problem at all. Understanding why they're not consuming reveals the real barriers.

### Integration Around the Job

Successful products integrate the entire experience around the job - not just the core function. Hiring a product includes the purchase experience, onboarding, ongoing use, and how it connects to adjacent jobs. Optimise the whole journey.

## Design Sprint (Jake Knapp)

A five-day process for answering critical business questions through design, prototyping, and testing. Compresses months of debate into one focused week.

### The Five-Day Structure

**Monday - Map and Target**
- Map the problem space end-to-end
- Interview internal experts (sales, support, engineering)
- Pick a specific target: which part of the map, which customer, which moment

**Tuesday - Sketch**
- Individual sketching (not group brainstorming - that produces groupthink)
- Lightning demos of existing solutions (not just competitors - any relevant inspiration)
- Four-step sketch: notes, ideas, crazy 8s, solution sketch
- Each person works alone. Diversity of ideas comes from independent thinking.

**Wednesday - Decide**
- Silent review of all sketches (no pitching)
- Dot voting, then discussion of clusters
- The Decider makes the final call (not consensus - one person decides)
- Storyboard the winning concept: scene by scene, 10-15 steps

**Thursday - Prototype**
- Build a realistic facade. It needs to look real enough to get honest reactions.
- Use real content, not lorem ipsum. Fake data kills honest feedback.
- Divide and conquer: makers, writers, stitchers, asset collectors
- It doesn't need to work. It needs to feel real for 60 minutes of testing.

**Friday - Test**
- Five customers, one-on-one, 60 minutes each
- Structured interview: context questions first, then prototype walkthrough
- The team watches via live stream, taking notes on a shared board
- Five is enough: surfaces ~85% of usability issues. Don't over-research.

### Key Principles

**The Decider**: One person with authority makes the final call when the team disagrees. This prevents design-by-committee and analysis paralysis. No Decider = no decision.

**Note and vote**: Silent individual work before group discussion. This prevents the loudest voice from dominating and produces genuinely diverse ideas.

**Realistic prototypes**: A prototype that looks fake gets fake reactions. Invest in making it feel real enough that people forget they're testing a prototype.

**Together alone**: People generate better ideas individually than in groups. Brainstorming feels productive but produces weaker ideas than structured individual work followed by structured critique.

### When to Sprint

Use a sprint when:
- You're about to invest significant engineering time on an unvalidated idea
- The team is stuck in analysis paralysis or circular debate
- There's a critical question that needs answering before you can move forward
- Stakeholders disagree on direction and need to see evidence

Don't sprint when:
- The solution is well-understood and just needs execution
- You don't have access to real customers for Friday testing
- The Decider won't participate or respect the outcome
- The scope is too small to warrant five days
