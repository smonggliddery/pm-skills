# Leadership

How to lead product teams, build trust, manage stakeholders, and drive change through people - not around them.

---

## Context Over Control (Marty Cagan)

The gap between the best and rest in product isn't talent - it's how leaders create the environment for product teams to do their best work.

### Empowered vs Feature Teams

**Feature teams** are handed solutions to implement. A stakeholder says "build this dashboard," the PM writes a spec for the dashboard, engineering builds the dashboard. The team never asks why. If the dashboard doesn't solve the problem, nobody feels responsible because they built what they were told.

**Empowered teams** own outcomes and have autonomy to solve problems. A leader says "our enterprise customers are churning because they can't get the insights they need." The team investigates, discovers the real issue, and builds the right solution - which might not be a dashboard at all.

### Product Leadership's Job

1. **Provide the strategic context**: Vision, strategy, objectives. Where are we going and why? What does winning look like?
2. **Set the constraints**: Timeline, resources, technical boundaries, business requirements. What are the guardrails?
3. **Trust the team**: Let them figure out the how. Don't prescribe solutions.
4. **Coach, don't direct**: The most impactful thing a product leader can do is coach their PMs, not hand them requirements.
5. **Remove blockers**: Clear the path. Fight the political battles. Shield the team from organizational noise.

### Product Strategy

A sequence of bets that connect vision to execution. Not a roadmap - a set of focused problems to solve.

- **Vision**: Compelling north star 3-10 years out. What does the world look like if we succeed?
- **Strategy**: The sequence of bets that gets us closer to the vision. Which problems, in what order?
- **Objectives**: Specific, measurable targets for the current period. How we'll know we're making progress.
- **Discovery**: The work teams do to figure out the best solution for each objective.

The leader owns vision and strategy. The team owns discovery and delivery.

### Coaching PMs

The PM coaching conversation:
- "What's the problem you're solving?" (not "what are you building?")
- "What evidence do you have?" (not "what do stakeholders want?")
- "What's your biggest risk?" (not "when will it ship?")
- "What have you ruled out and why?" (not "is this the best option?")
- "What would change your mind?" (not "how confident are you?")

### Stakeholder Management

Transform stakeholders from demanding features to trusting outcomes:
- **Share evidence, not just conclusions**: "Here's what we learned from 15 customer interviews" not "we've decided to build X"
- **Invite them into discovery**: Let them watch customer interviews. Share research findings. Make them feel the problem.
- **Demonstrate learning**: Regular updates on what you're learning, not just what you're building
- **Deliver results**: Nothing builds trust like shipping something that moves the metric they care about

## BRAVING Trust (Brene Brown)

Trust is built in small moments, not grand gestures. The BRAVING framework gives you a vocabulary for talking about trust concretely.

### The Seven Elements

**Boundaries**: You respect my boundaries, and when you're unsure, you ask. I do the same. In product: "I need you to tell me when a request is outside your team's scope, not quietly absorb it."

**Reliability**: You do what you say you'll do. Not sometimes - consistently. In product: commit to what you can actually deliver. Under-promise, over-deliver.

**Accountability**: You own your mistakes, apologise, and make amends. I do the same. In product: "That launch went badly and here's what I'm changing" not "the data was misleading."

**Vault**: You don't share information or experiences that aren't yours to share. In product: what's said in retros stays in retros. Customer interview data is treated with respect.

**Integrity**: You choose courage over comfort. You practise your values rather than just professing them. In product: push back on a bad feature request even when the stakeholder is powerful.

**Non-judgment**: I can ask for help without being judged. You can struggle in front of me without being judged. In product: it's safe to say "I don't know" or "I was wrong."

**Generosity**: You extend the most generous interpretation possible to my intentions and actions. In product: assume good faith first. That stakeholder demanding a feature is probably trying to solve a real problem, even if their solution is wrong.

## Vulnerability in Leadership (Brene Brown)

Vulnerability is not weakness. It's the birthplace of innovation, creativity, and change. Leaders who avoid vulnerability avoid growth.

### Armoured vs Daring Leadership

| Armoured | Daring |
|---|---|
| Perfectionism | Healthy striving and self-compassion |
| Knowing everything | Curiosity and learning |
| Never wrong | "I was wrong, here's what I learned" |
| Using power over people | Using power with people |
| Cynicism | Modelling clarity, kindness, hope |
| Comfort zone | Courage zone |

### Clear Is Kind

Avoiding difficult feedback isn't kindness - it's avoidance. Being unclear about expectations, avoiding tough conversations, and dancing around problems creates more pain long-term.

- "I need to give you some feedback about the spec quality" is kinder than silently rewriting their work
- "This timeline isn't realistic and here's why" is kinder than letting the team commit to something impossible
- "I disagree with this direction because..." is kinder than agreeing in the meeting and undermining after

### Living Into Values

Identify your two core values and operationalise them:
- What do they look like in practice? (Not "I value integrity" - "When a stakeholder asks me to hide bad data, I show it and explain why")
- What behaviours support these values?
- What behaviours contradict them?
- Where are you being tested right now?

## Change Management (Chip Heath & Dan Heath)

Change fails not because people are lazy but because there's a conflict between the rational mind (the Rider), the emotional mind (the Elephant), and the environment (the Path).

### Direct the Rider (Rational Mind)

The Rider is analytical but prone to overthinking. Give the Rider clear direction.

**Find the bright spots**: What's already working? Instead of analysing everything that's wrong, find the positive deviant - the team, process, or behaviour that's succeeding against the odds - and replicate it.

**Script the critical moves**: Don't think big, think specific. "In the next sprint, run one customer interview before writing any acceptance criteria" is better than "become more customer-centric." Ambiguity is the enemy of change.

**Point to the destination**: Show where you're heading and why it's worth it. "In six months, our specs will be so good that engineering never comes back with clarifying questions in the first week" gives the Rider a clear picture to work toward.

### Motivate the Elephant (Emotional Mind)

The Elephant is powerful but easily spooked. Logic alone won't move it.

**Find the feeling**: Knowing isn't enough - people need to feel the need for change. Show them the customer interview recording where someone struggles. Let them feel the pain. Data informs, emotion motivates.

**Shrink the change**: Make it small enough to not be scary. "Run one customer interview this week" not "transform our discovery process." Small wins create momentum. Stack them.

**Grow your people**: Cultivate identity. "We're the kind of team that talks to customers every week" is more powerful than "our process requires weekly interviews." People act consistently with their identity. Change the identity, change the behaviour.

### Shape the Path (Environment)

When the Path is clear, even reluctant people follow it.

**Tweak the environment**: Make the right behaviour the easy behaviour. Put the customer interview template in the project template. Add "evidence" as a required field in the ticket template. Reduce friction for the behaviour you want.

**Build habits**: Use triggers and routines. "Every Monday morning, review last week's customer feedback" becomes automatic. Habits don't require motivation or willpower.

**Rally the herd**: Behaviour is contagious. Make the desired behaviour visible. Share customer interview clips in Slack. Celebrate when someone cites evidence in a spec review. Show that "people like us do things like this."

### When to Use This

- Introducing new processes (discovery practices, spec standards, review cadences)
- Driving adoption of new tools or ways of working
- Changing team culture or norms
- Getting stakeholder buy-in that's stalling despite good arguments
- Any situation where the logical case is clear but action isn't following
