# Communication

How to make ideas stick, write clearly, and translate across audiences. Communication is not about what you say - it's about what lands.

---

## The SUCCESs Framework (Chip Heath & Dan Heath)

Some ideas stick and others don't. Sticky ideas share six principles. Understanding these lets you craft messages that people remember and act on.

### Simple

Find the core. Strip to the essential message. If you could only tell them one thing, what would it be?

**Commander's Intent**: The military uses this because plans fall apart on contact with reality. What's the one thing that must happen even if everything else changes? That's your core message.

- "We're building search for large teams" not "We're enhancing the team management experience with intelligent filtering and discovery capabilities"
- Force yourself to prioritise. If everything is important, nothing is.
- Simple does not mean dumbed down. It means finding the essence and communicating it crisply.

### Unexpected

Break patterns to get attention. Use surprise to get them to look up, then curiosity to keep them engaged.

- **Gap theory**: Curiosity happens when people feel a gap between what they know and what they want to know. Open the gap first ("We're losing 40% of task assignments mid-flow"), then fill it.
- **Break the script**: If every sprint review starts with velocity charts, start with a customer quote instead.
- Don't be surprising for its own sake. The surprise must connect to the core message.

### Concrete

Use sensory language and specific details, not abstractions. People remember "a team admin scrolling through 150 names looking for Sarah" more than "suboptimal user experience in member selection."

- Use real examples, real names, real numbers
- Show, don't tell. Walk through a scenario, don't describe a category.
- The more abstract the concept, the more concrete the example needs to be

### Credible

Make it believable. Use:
- **Internal credibility**: Specific details signal you've done the work. "We interviewed 12 team admins across 5 companies" beats "based on research."
- **External credibility**: Data, but make it human-scale. "40% of admins abandon the assignment flow" not "the assignment completion rate is 60%."
- **Testable credentials**: "Try assigning a task in a 150-person team and see how long it takes you." Let them verify the claim.

### Emotional

Make people feel something. Information makes people think. Emotion makes people act.

- **Appeal to self-interest**: "This will save your team 10 hours a week" not "this improves operational efficiency"
- **Appeal to identity**: "This is what great product teams do" not "this is a best practice"
- Connect to individual stories, not statistics. One customer's frustration is more persuasive than aggregate data.

### Stories

Stories are flight simulators for the brain. They help people simulate situations and inspire action.

Three types of stories that work:
1. **Challenge plot**: Protagonist overcomes obstacle (customer struggling with the current system)
2. **Connection plot**: People bridging a gap (cross-team collaboration unlocked by a feature)
3. **Creativity plot**: Mental breakthrough (the insight that led to this approach)

Use stories to make abstract strategy concrete: "When Priya at Acme Corp tried to assign a bug to the right engineer last Tuesday, she scrolled for 90 seconds, gave up, and assigned it to herself."

## The Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something, you can't imagine not knowing it. This makes experts terrible communicators. You've been living with this problem for weeks - your audience is hearing about it for the first time.

### How It Shows Up

- Using jargon without realising it's jargon
- Skipping context because it's "obvious"
- Assuming your audience cares about the same things you care about
- Being frustrated when people "don't get it" (they don't have your context)

### How to Fight It

- **Test with outsiders**: Show your message to someone unfamiliar with the project. Where do they get confused?
- **Start with their context, not yours**: What do they already know? What do they care about? Start there.
- **Use concrete examples**: Abstraction is where the curse hides. Specifics are harder to misunderstand.
- **Name the gap**: "You might not know that..." or "Background you'll need..." signals that you respect their time.

## Writing Clearly (Ann Handley)

Writing is thinking made visible. If you can't write it clearly, you haven't thought it through clearly.

### Core Principles

**Start with empathy**: Begin with what the reader needs, not what you want to say. Every document should answer the reader's first question: "Why should I care?"

**The ugly first draft**: Get it down, then get it good. Don't edit while you write. Separate creation (messy, fast) from refinement (precise, slow).

**Brevity and clarity**: Shorter sentences. Simpler words. Kill the jargon. If a 10-year-old can't understand the sentence structure, simplify it. (The content can be sophisticated - the language shouldn't be.)

**Show, don't tell**: "Customers scroll for 90 seconds and give up" not "the experience is suboptimal." Specific details and real examples beat abstract claims every time.

**Voice matters**: Write like a human, not a brand. Be warm, direct, useful. Would you say this sentence to someone's face? If not, rewrite it.

### Practical Rules

- Lead with the conclusion. Put the "so what" in the first sentence, not the last.
- One idea per paragraph. If a paragraph has two ideas, it's two paragraphs.
- Use active voice. "We decided" not "It was decided."
- Cut adverbs. If you need "very" or "really," the verb isn't strong enough.
- Read it aloud. If you stumble, the reader will too.

## Audience Translation

The same information needs different framing for different audiences. What matters to them is different from what matters to you.

### For Engineering

- Lead with the problem, not the business case
- Be technically precise about requirements
- Specify constraints, not solutions (unless the solution is the constraint)
- Include edge cases, error handling, dependencies
- Respect their time - be concise and skimmable

### For Executives

- Lead with the outcome and business impact
- One page maximum. If it's longer, it's not clear enough.
- Three things: what's happening, why it matters, what you need from them
- Use numbers they care about (revenue, customers, market position)
- Anticipate their questions and answer them preemptively

### For Customers

- Lead with what changes for them
- Use their language, not your internal terminology
- Focus on benefit, not mechanism ("you can now find team members instantly" not "we implemented fuzzy search with debounced input handling")
- Be honest about limitations and timelines
- Tell them what to do next

### For Sales and Support

- Lead with what they can now tell customers
- Include the exact language they should use
- Anticipate customer questions and provide answers
- Explain what's NOT changing (they get asked about everything)
- Give them the timeline and rollout plan

### Translation Checklist

For any message, before sending:
- [ ] Have I started with what matters to THEM, not me?
- [ ] Am I using their language, not mine?
- [ ] Have I cut everything they don't need?
- [ ] Have I anticipated their first two questions?
- [ ] Would this make sense if they read nothing else about the topic?
